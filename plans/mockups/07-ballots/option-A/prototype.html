Working Group: IoT Security • Chair: Sarah Chen
23 Pending 45 Resolved
IoT Security Standard
Version 2.0 Draft 3 • January 2026

11. Introduction

2 This standard establishes security requirements for Internet of Things (IoT) devices deployed in enterprise environments.
3 It applies to all connected devices that collect, process, or transmit sensitive data. 1

52. Scope

6 This standard covers the following device categories:
7 • Industrial sensors and actuators
8 • Smart building systems 2
9 • Healthcare monitoring devices
10 • Network infrastructure Edge computing devices ✓

123. Security Requirements

13 3.1 Authentication
14 All IoT devices SHALL implement multi-factor authentication for administrative access. 3
15 Default passwords MUST be changed during initial device setup.
16 Devices SHALL support certificate-based authentication for machine-to-machine communication. 4
18 3.2 Encryption
19 Data in transit MUST be encrypted using TLS 1.3 or higher.
20 Data at rest SHOULD be encrypted using AES-256 or equivalent. 5

224. Compliance and Certification

23 Manufacturers SHALL provide documentation demonstrating compliance with this standard.
24 Third-party security audits MUST be conducted annually for certified devices. 6

Comments & Resolution

Michael Torres
Line 3 • Section 1
Technical
The phrase "sensitive data" is too broad. We need to define what constitutes sensitive data in the context of IoT devices. Suggest adding a definition section.
Suggested: "...that collect, process, or transmit data classified as Confidential or higher per the organization's data classification policy."
Lisa Park
Line 8 • Section 2
Editorial
Consider adding "HVAC and environmental controls" as a specific example under smart building systems for clarity.
David Kim
Line 10 • Section 2
Resolved
"Network infrastructure" is too broad and overlaps with traditional IT security standards. Suggest replacing with "Edge computing devices" to better reflect IoT context.
Resolution: Accepted. Changed to "Edge computing devices" as suggested.
Jennifer Wong
Line 14 • Section 3.1
Technical
MFA requirement may not be practical for all IoT device types, especially headless devices. Suggest changing SHALL to SHOULD with exception criteria.
Robert Martinez
Line 16 • Section 3.1
Editorial
Good addition. Suggest also mentioning key rotation requirements for certificate-based auth.
Amanda Foster
Line 20 • Section 3.2
Technical
SHOULD is too weak for data at rest encryption. Many IoT devices handle sensitive health or financial data. Recommend changing to MUST with exceptions for resource-constrained devices.
Thomas Anderson
Line 24 • Section 4
Editorial
Annual audits may be too frequent and costly. Consider biennial audits with mandatory security patch verification between audit cycles.
